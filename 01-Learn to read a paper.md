# Notes — Learned to Read a Research Paper
## 08/04/2026

After going through the five materials, I realized that my previous understanding of reading a research paper was quite wrong. I used to think that reading means understanding every detail, including formulas, proofs, and all technical aspects. However, now I understand that the real goal is not to master everything, but to extract the core idea, understand the contribution, and think beyond what is written.

One of the most important insights I gained is the concept of reading in multiple passes. Instead of forcing myself to read line by line from beginning to end, I should approach a paper strategically. In the first pass, I only need to get a general understanding by reading the title, abstract, introduction, and conclusion. This helps me quickly decide whether the paper is relevant or not. Then, in the second pass, I focus on understanding the main idea, the method, and the results, especially through figures and diagrams. Only if the paper is truly important do I move to the third pass, where I try to deeply understand the logic and even mentally reconstruct the work as if I were the author.

Another key lesson is that I should always read with a clear purpose. Not every paper deserves deep reading. Sometimes, skimming is enough. Sometimes, I only need to understand the main idea. And only in certain cases do I need to fully analyze the paper. This mindset helps me manage time better and avoid getting stuck on unnecessary details.

What really changed my perspective is how I should think while reading. I should not passively accept what the authors say. Instead, I need to be critical by questioning whether the problem is meaningful, whether the assumptions are valid, and whether the conclusions are justified. At the same time, I should also think creatively. I should ask myself how the idea can be improved, extended, or applied to other contexts. This is where reading transforms into research thinking.

I also learned that reading should be an active process. Taking notes, highlighting important parts, and writing down questions are essential. One simple but powerful technique is to summarize the paper in one or two sentences. If I can do that clearly, it means I truly understand the core idea of the paper.

Perhaps the most important realization is that research ideas do not come out of nowhere. They come from reading many papers, comparing them, and identifying gaps or unexplored areas. This means that reading is not just preparation for research—it is part of the research process itself.

Finally, I now understand that I should not rely too much on my advisor for detailed guidance. The expectation is that I can learn independently, explore papers on my own, and gradually develop my own research direction. This requires discipline, curiosity, and consistent practice.

---

## **Key Takeaways**

- Reading papers is about understanding ideas, not memorizing details  
- The three-pass method is essential for efficient reading  
- Always read with a clear purpose to save time  
- Critical and creative thinking are both required  
- Active reading (notes, summaries) improves understanding  
- Research ideas come from comparing and extending existing work  
- Independent learning is a key expectation in research
